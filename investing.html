Investing Philosophy

My interest in financial markets began early, shaped by family influences and experiences during the Global Financial Crisis. I've been personally involved in the stock and options markets, trading my own capital, for roughly 20 years. While I've read widely on investing and trading, I am not a professional money manager or trader. What the process has given me is a fairly honest understanding of myself—my strengths, and more importantly, my weaknesses when it comes to patience, risk tolerance, and conviction. Markets have a way of being very effective mirrors.

LLY — Eli Lilly 21.12%

Thesis: I remain deeply optimistic about the long-term potential of Eli Lilly and Company and the broader GLP-1 therapeutic category. The United States is facing one of the largest public health crises in modern history: according to the CDC, more than 40% of American adults are considered obese, while roughly 9% are classified as severely obese. The downstream effects on society are enormous, with obesity-related healthcare costs estimated at approximately $173 billion annually in direct medical spending alone.

What makes this opportunity especially compelling to me is that GLP-1 therapies may ultimately prove to be far more important than "weight-loss drugs." Early clinical and observational research suggests these medications may positively affect the brain's reward pathways, potentially reducing compulsive and addictive behaviors tied to alcohol, nicotine, opioids, and other substances. Researchers are increasingly studying whether GLP-1 therapies can help patients struggling with alcohol dependence, impulsive behavior, and other co-morbidities that quietly diminish quality of life for millions of people.

If these findings continue to hold up in larger clinical trials, I believe GLP-1 drugs could evolve into one of the most significant pharmaceutical platforms of the next several decades — not simply by extending life expectancy, but by improving daily functioning, self-control, metabolic health, and overall quality of life for ordinary people. In my view, this possibility materially expands the long-term opportunity set for Eli Lilly beyond obesity and diabetes alone.

Idea from: Stansberry Investor Hour, Dan Ferris. I still need to recall the analyst guest's name from a 2022 or 2023 episode on GLP-1 drug prospects.

TLN — Talen Energy 17.14%
  • Short call: Jan 2028 $350 Call 2.46%

Thesis: My investment thesis for Talen Energy centers on a simple belief: reliable electricity will become one of the most strategically important resources of the next several decades, and nuclear power is one of the few scalable solutions capable of supporting modern civilization at industrial scale.

Talen Energy is the majority owner of the Susquehanna nuclear power plant in Pennsylvania and has signed long-term nuclear energy agreements with Amazon Web Services (AWS) to support the growing power demands of AI and hyperscale data centers.

Based on my reading of global energy trends, there is no realistic pathway to materially reducing humanity's environmental footprint without nuclear power. At the same time, the United States appears to be moving toward a significant energy shortage driven by AI infrastructure, electrification, manufacturing reshoring, and rising electricity demand.

While political inertia may delay meaningful action until the problem becomes acute, I would rather be an owner of existing strategic energy assets before scarcity is fully recognized by the market.

Idea from: Enrique Abeyta, Whitney Tilson, Erik Townsend

NAT — Nordic American Tankers 8.91%

Thesis: My investment thesis for Nordic American Tankers is rooted in a simple reality: despite decades of discussion around energy transition, petroleum products remain among mankind's primary energy resources, and the global economy still depends heavily on the ability to move energy safely and efficiently around the world.

I have been fascinated by shipping and petroleum infrastructure since college. The scale of engineering, logistics, and long-term planning required to transport energy globally has an enormous and often overlooked impact on everyday life and economic stability.

Peter Zeihan's book, The End of the World Is Just the Beginning, helped reinforce this perspective by highlighting how secure global shipping lanes enabled much of the economic growth of the 20th and early 21st centuries. As the first quarter of the 21st century comes to a close, many of those geopolitical assumptions appear increasingly fragile. Events affecting critical shipping chokepoints such as the Strait of Hormuz continue to demonstrate how sensitive the global economy remains to disruptions in energy transportation.

Nordic American Tankers operates a fleet of approximately 20 Suezmax crude oil tankers focused on transporting petroleum globally. In my view, companies with existing shipping infrastructure and operational scale may continue to benefit from persistent global energy demand and the increasing geopolitical complexity surrounding energy transportation.

Idea from: Peter Zeihan

LPG — Dorian LPG 8.39%

Thesis: My investment thesis for Dorian LPG is closely related to my broader views on global energy transportation and shipping infrastructure. The United States possesses enormous proven natural gas reserves that can be economically accessed through hydraulic fracturing — a geographic advantage that many countries in Europe and Asia simply do not have.

As global energy demand continues to rise, I believe the United States stands to benefit significantly from exporting liquefied petroleum gas and natural gas products to regions with fewer domestic energy resources. In many ways, America's energy abundance is one of its greatest long-term strategic advantages.

Dorian LPG operates modern VLGC (Very Large Gas Carrier) vessels that help transport these energy products globally. In my view, companies involved in the movement of energy — particularly those with existing infrastructure and specialized fleets — may continue to benefit from the growing importance of global energy security and international trade.

Idea from: Peter Zeihan

TJX — TJX Companies 6.13%

My family — especially the women in my life — have always been loyal TJ Maxx customers. Having grown up in a working-class household, I think that kind of long-term brand loyalty says a great deal about the staying power of the company and its connection with everyday consumers.

Quarterly Review

Wednesday, April 1, 2026

Q1 2026 (January 1, 2026 through March 31, 2026): my retirement accounts gained approximately 2.0% for the quarter, versus a −4.6% return for the S&P 500, for about 6.6 percentage points of outperformance.

At the end of Q1 2026, my retirement accounts were a useful reminder that portfolio results are usually driven by a handful of positions, not by everything moving in the same direction at once.

My best performers were Nordic American Tankers (NAT) and Dorian LPG (LPG), with Generac (GNRC) also contributing meaningfully. These positions did a lot of the heavy lifting during the quarter and helped offset weakness elsewhere in the portfolio.

My largest drags were Eli Lilly (LLY) and Talen Energy (TLN). That was not just because they declined, but because they were meaningful positions. Position size matters. A modest move in a large holding usually matters more than a large move in a small one.

One thing that stood out this quarter was the value of selling covered calls. In higher-multiple stocks especially, covered calls can be an effective way to cushion against drawdowns. They do not eliminate downside, and they cap some upside, but they can help reduce volatility and generate income while holding through periods of weakness.

More broadly, one of the more durable lessons I have taken from value investors is that building a sustainable portfolio is not really about chasing outperformance in every quarter. In my view, the higher achievement is often much simpler and much harder: when the benchmark is down, be down by less. Compounding is helped not only by upside, but by avoiding unnecessary damage on the downside. To borrow one of Charlie Munger’s recurring ideas, if you can avoid the big mistakes, the favorable outcomes tend to take care of themselves. In that respect, I am pleased with my results this quarter.

A few new positions also appeared in the portfolio this quarter, including IGV, ASML, and SCCO. They are still relatively small, but they reflect a bit more diversification in the current mix.

The broader lesson for me is simple: performance is rarely about being “right” in the abstract. It is about where capital was actually allocated, how concentrated those bets were, and whether the winners were large enough to matter. Just as importantly, it is about having a few tools available to manage risk when the market turns against you.
